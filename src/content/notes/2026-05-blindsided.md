There's a particular kind of professional anxiety that comes with being close enough to the decisions to see them coming. I'd spent enough time as a leader at my company to understand what the numbers meant, to watch the tactics shift, and to recognize the pattern of a small organization trying to outrun a problem it couldn't quite name. Sales were underperforming. Runway was the unspoken word in every room. I knew, in the way you know things you'd rather not, that layoffs were possible — and that a non-billable leader at a struggling consultancy was a reasonable place to start.

What I didn't know — what I couldn't know, despite all of that awareness — was how much it was going to hurt anyway.

---

The company was small. I privately called it "boutique," which is a generous word for an organization held together by talented people, good intentions, and optimism that was slowly running out. I'd been promoted to lead a team there, and it was some of the best work of my career. Not because everything ran smoothly — it rarely did — but because the human part of the work was genuinely good. I was mentoring people I cared about. I was running interference so my team could do their best work. I was building something, incrementally, with real people.

That's the thing about leadership that doesn't show up cleanly on a résumé: it's relational at its core. You're not just managing tasks and shipping deliverables. You're paying attention to people — to what they need, what's getting in their way, where they want to go. You're the one absorbing the friction so they don't have to. When it works, it's genuinely one of the more satisfying things you can do professionally. When it gets taken from you, what you lose isn't just a job title. It's the practice. The people. The daily work of giving a damn.

When I was let go in January, the professional logic was clear to me. The company was struggling. I wasn't billable. The math was simple, and it wasn't a verdict on what I was capable of. I knew that. I still know it.

But knowing a thing and feeling a thing are different problems entirely.

---

Within a month of my departure, a lost account sent the company into a tailspin that resulted in the furlough of most of my remaining team. In a strange way, that was clarifying. It confirmed what I'd suspected — that what happened wasn't about me, it was about a company coming apart at the seams. For a few of the individuals whose decisions had led to that moment, I won't pretend I didn't feel some private vindication. I'm only human.

But mostly what I felt was sad. The company at its best had been a genuinely good place, one where I'd done work I was proud of alongside people I liked. Watching it come apart didn't feel like winning anything. It felt like the end of something that deserved a better ending.

My instinct, almost immediately, was to shift into support mode for the colleagues who'd been left behind in that mess. That came naturally. They were my people, and they were now dealing with their own version of a shitty situation I'd just lived through. I wanted to help, and I did.

What I didn't do was slow down long enough to deal with my own experience.

---

Here's what I'd tell anyone going through something similar: the practical urgency of a layoff can trick you out of processing it.

I had a family to support and a mortgage and all of the material reasons to move quickly. I'd seen the writing on the wall early enough to start a quiet job search before the axe fell. I had a head start. So instead of giving myself a week or two to just sit in the feeling — to acknowledge that something real had been taken from me and that it was okay to be affected by that — I shifted the job search from a casual exploration into a full-contact sprint.

That's not irrational. In fact, it's probably the responsible choice if you're thinking purely about household stability. But I paid for it later. The grief didn't disappear just because I didn't schedule it. It just got deferred, and it showed up sideways in the months that followed — in the way I was making decisions, in how I was framing what I wanted next, in a job choice I made too quickly that turned out to be a misstep. That's a different story for a different day. But its roots were here.

There's also something specific about being a leader that makes this harder to navigate. Leadership roles carry a particular weight of identity. You're not just a person with a job — you're the person your team comes to. The one with the read on the situation. The one moving toward the problems rather than away from them. When that gets removed suddenly, you're left with a question you didn't necessarily know you'd been avoiding: *who are you when nobody needs you to have the answer?*

I'm also aware that "middle management" is practically a punchline right now, which adds its own layer of indignity. At the exact moment you're most vulnerable and most need to articulate your value clearly, you're trying to make the case for a kind of work that a lot of people have decided is either unnecessary or replaceable. The work of building people up, of creating real human connection in a team, of blocking and tackling so others can thrive — that's genuinely hard, and genuinely valuable, and it doesn't always compress into a bullet point that lands well on a resume. Having to justify it to a hiring manager while you're also quietly processing a loss is its own special challenge.

---

It's been about a year and a half since all of this happened. I've had time to get angry, to resolve things internally, and to mostly make peace with it. The CEO and I are still acquaintances — our daughters are in the same class at school, because apparently the universe has a sense of humor — and that's introduced its own set of interpersonal wrinkles along the way.

What I know now is this: you can be analytically prepared for something and emotionally blindsided by it at the same time. The two aren't mutually exclusive. Reading the room correctly doesn't immunize you against the loss when the thing you feared actually happens. Competence and grief can coexist.

And if you're in the middle of it right now — watching the writing on the wall, or having just gotten the call, or staring at a job search that suddenly has real stakes — I'd offer this: let yourself feel the thing. The urgency is real, and you'll have to deal with it. But so is the loss. Make a little room for both.

The practical stuff has a way of working itself out. The unprocessed stuff has a way of following you until you deal with it.
